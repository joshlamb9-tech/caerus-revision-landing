# Caerus Revision — Market Research
## Channels, Communities, and Pricing

*Produced: March 2026. Applies to Caerus Latin launch; principles carry across French and Maths.*

---

## Part 1: Where to Market Online

### The Targeting Problem

There are three distinct audiences and they live in different places. Treating them as one will waste effort. Be clear on who you're talking to before picking a channel.

| Audience | Who They Are | Where They Are |
|---|---|---|
| **Heads of Classics / Latin teachers** | ~500 IAPS prep schools, most have one Classics teacher | The Classics Library, ARLT, Classical Association, TES, LinkedIn |
| **Prep school parents** | Fee-paying, motivated, anxious, price-insensitive | Mumsnet, Facebook groups, Good Schools Guide, word of mouth |
| **CE Latin tutors** | Freelance tutors preparing pupils for CE 13+ | Tutorful, MyTutor, tutor networks, ARLT tutor listings |

The fastest path to revenue is **Heads of Classics** (school licences). The highest volume path over time is **parents** (individual access). Tutors are a secondary channel that may generate organic word of mouth if the tool is genuinely useful.

---

### Channel 1: The Classics Library

**URL:** theclassicslibrary.com
**Reach:** 2,000+ members, stated to be "the vast majority practising teachers in the UK"
**Cost:** Paid advertising available (exact rates not published — email to enquire)
**Effort:** Low
**Verdict: Priority 1 for the teacher channel.**

This is TES but specifically for Classics teachers. The site description says explicitly: "With over two thousand members, the vast majority of whom are practising teachers in the UK, an advertisement here targets perfectly the most desirable audience." That is a direct statement that paid advertising is available and worthwhile.

Action: Email the site owner (Stephen Jenkin — @StephenJenkin on X) and ask about advertising rates and whether a free listing or review is possible as a new resource. He runs the site himself; it's a personal project by a practising teacher. A peer-to-peer pitch will land better than a commercial one.

### Channel 2: ARLT — Association for Latin Teaching

**URL:** arlt.co.uk
**Reach:** National membership of Latin and Classics teachers; runs CPD days, summer school, refresher day
**Cost:** Free to submit resources; membership required for some areas
**Effort:** Low-medium
**Verdict: Priority 2 for teachers. Submit Caerus Latin as a free resource.**

ARLT maintains a resource library for members and has a private tutor listings page. The organisation is run by practising teachers for teachers — exactly the audience. Their events (Summer School in July, Refresher Day in March) are attended by CE prep school teachers alongside GCSE and A-Level teachers.

Actions:
- Submit Caerus Latin to their resources section
- Become an ARLT member personally (low cost, gives credibility and access)
- Consider sponsoring or presenting at their annual Refresher Day — even a 5-minute slot as "a teacher showing what they've built" is plausible and would put the tool in front of the exact audience

### Channel 3: The Classical Association

**URL:** classicalassociation.org
**Reach:** National subject association; CPD days, regional events, Journal of Classics Teaching
**Cost:** Membership required; advertising in journal is paid
**Effort:** Medium
**Verdict: Longer term — useful for credibility, not for immediate sales.**

The Classical Association is more academic in tone than ARLT. It is worth being a member and being visible in their community, but it is unlikely to generate direct sales quickly. Their Facebook page (for the Journal of Classics Teaching) has an engaged following of Classics educators and is worth engaging with organically — share useful content, not ads.

### Channel 4: Mumsnet

**URL:** mumsnet.com/talk/education and mumsnet.com/talk/private-school
**Reach:** 6 million+ monthly unique visitors; dedicated private school and CE threads
**Cost:** Free (organic posting) — paid advertising exists but is expensive and blunt
**Effort:** Medium (requires participation, not just posting a link)
**Verdict: Priority 1 for the parent channel. But do not advertise — contribute.**

Mumsnet has multiple active threads specifically about Common Entrance revision resources. Parents ask "what resources are good for CE Latin?" with no satisfactory answer in most threads. This is a direct opportunity.

The right approach is to find those threads and post as a teacher who has built a tool: "I'm a CE Latin teacher at an independent prep school and built this revision site for my own Year 8 pupils — some parents have found it useful." This is genuine, credible, and not promotional in tone. Mumsnet users are hostile to overt marketing but receptive to recommendations from people with evident expertise.

Specific threads to look for and contribute to:
- "Common Entrance best resources to prepare" (recurring thread)
- "13+ common entrance — where do we even start with revision?"
- "Latin at prep school — what helps?"
- The private-school board is active with exactly the parents who would pay £25–35 for an individual licence

Do not post a link and disappear. Spend 20 minutes in the thread, answer other questions, then mention the tool in context. Return if people reply.

### Channel 5: Facebook Groups

Public search does not surface specific named groups cleanly — Facebook's search is poor from outside the platform. However, the following types of groups exist and are worth searching for directly on Facebook:

**Groups to find and join:**
- "Common Entrance 13+" (search this exact phrase on Facebook)
- "Eton Prep / Winchester prep / Harrow prep" parent groups — each major senior school has parent prep groups
- "ISEB Common Pre-Test" groups (parents preparing for the pre-test at 11+ often continue in CE groups)
- "CE Parents 13+" or similar
- Home education groups that include CE pupils (a small but real segment)

**Tutor-focused groups:**
- "Private Tutors UK" — active group of freelance tutors; posting a resource they can recommend is legitimate
- "Classics and Latin Teachers UK" — search for this type of group

**Reach:** Highly variable, typically 500–5,000 members for school-specific groups; national CE groups may be larger.
**Cost:** Free
**Effort:** Low to find, medium to use well
**Verdict:** Worth 30 minutes of Facebook searching to identify 3–5 relevant groups. Post once in each with the teacher framing. Do not repost repeatedly.

### Channel 7: TES

**URL:** tes.com
**Reach:** 13 million+ registered teachers globally; large UK Classics section
**Cost:** Free to list resources; TES takes a cut of paid resources
**Effort:** Low-medium
**Verdict:** List a free taster resource on TES with a link to Caerus Latin.**

TES has a large Latin/Classics section and teachers actively browse it for resources. A well-made free resource (e.g. a vocab quiz PDF, a grammar reference sheet) with "more at caerusrevision.co.uk" is a legitimate and commonly-used funnel. This is how many EdTech products get discovered by teachers — they find a free resource, like it, and look up the creator.

Do not put the full product on TES. Put a genuinely useful taster that demonstrates quality and drives traffic to the site.

### Channel 8: Tutor Platforms (Tutorful, MyTutor, Superprof)

**Can Caerus be listed directly?** No — these are tutor matching platforms, not resource directories. You cannot list a website as a product.

**Indirect route — tutor word of mouth:**
Tutors who teach CE Latin are a small but influential group. If a tutor recommends a resource to 10 families, that is 10 potential individual licence sales. The route is:

1. Find CE Latin tutors on Tutorful (search "Latin Common Entrance") — there are typically 20–40 listed at any given time
2. ARLT maintains a private tutor listings page — tutors registered there are self-identified CE Latin specialists
3. Email or message 10–15 tutors directly: "I've built a CE Latin revision site used at my school — thought it might be useful for your pupils. Happy for you to share it with families."

This is not scalable, but it requires two hours and could generate 20+ individual licence sales in a term if the tutors find it genuinely useful.

**Cost:** Free
**Effort:** Medium (manual outreach)
**Verdict:** Worth doing once Caerus Latin is live and has proven useful with a few users.

### Channel 9: LinkedIn

**Relevant groups and contacts:**
- IAPS page (Independent Association of Prep Schools) — 3,000+ followers, mostly heads and senior staff
- ISA (Independent Schools Association) — 8,600+ followers
- Search "Head of Classics" in UK — you will find a handful of relevant profiles

**Reality check:** LinkedIn is not the right primary channel here. Prep school teachers — especially Classics teachers — are not heavily active on LinkedIn. The decision-maker for adopting a Latin revision tool is the Head of Classics, and they are more likely to be found at an ARLT event or on X than on LinkedIn.

The one strong LinkedIn use case: if you are building the career narrative (Director of Digital Learning, Deputy Head Academic), LinkedIn is where you document what you've built. Post about Caerus Latin as a product launch. Tag IAPS. This serves the career goal more than the sales goal.

**Cost:** Free
**Effort:** Low
**Verdict:** Worth a post at launch for career narrative. Not a primary sales channel.

### Channel 10: IAPS and ISA Directories

**IAPS** represents 660+ preparatory schools. They do not operate a public directory of teachers or Heads of Department. Their member communications (newsletter, CPD emails) go to headteachers and bursars, not subject teachers.

**How to use IAPS:** Not as a marketing channel directly. Rather, once Caerus has 2–3 paying schools, frame this as an IAPS-community success story. Heads talk to other heads. The word "another IAPS school is using it" is more persuasive than any advertisement.

**ISA:** Smaller body (500+ schools); similar structure. Less useful than IAPS for CE specifically as ISA includes some non-CE schools.

## Part 2: Pricing for Individual Users

### The Competitive Landscape

Understanding what comparable platforms charge sets the ceiling and floor. The key insight is that Caerus is not competing with these products — it is operating in a different niche. But the prices parents are already paying set their expectations.

**Mass-market GCSE platforms (what parents know):**

| Platform | Model | Price | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Save My Exams | Freemium + subscription | £48/yr annual, £30/3 months, £12/month | Full access all subjects; GCSE and A-Level focus |
| Seneca Learning | Freemium + premium | ~£108/yr (annual); free tier available | Large content library; primarily GCSE |
| Tassomai | Subscription (by year group) | £29.99/month for Years 7–8; £44.99/month for Years 9–11 | AI-driven retrieval; GCSE science focus |
| Atom Learning | Subscription | £48–£70/month (monthly); £32–£56/month (annual) | 11+ and entrance exam prep; well-positioned for prep school parents |
| CGP books | One-off purchase | £6–£12 per book | Physical revision guides; no digital equivalent |
| Galore Park (CE Latin) | One-off per book | £12–£18 per book | The incumbent print resource for CE Latin |

**What this tells you:**

1. Parents already paying £29–45/month for Tassomai or Atom Learning are accustomed to spending £350–500+/year on revision tools. A £25–35 charge for a specialist CE Latin tool looks cheap by comparison.

2. The Save My Exams model (£48/year, all subjects) anchors a "whole-year all-in subscription" at under £50. If you price above this, you need a clear differentiation story — which you have: CE-specific, not GCSE; built by a practising CE teacher, not a content factory.

3. Galore Park sells a CE Latin revision book for £12–18. Parents who have already bought one or two Galore Park guides are primed to spend money on CE Latin resources and understand that niche products cost more than general ones.

4. Atom Learning's pricing (£30–56/month annualised) shows that parents will pay significant amounts for quality entrance exam prep tools. Caerus is not Atom Learning in scope, but the price sensitivity benchmarked here is encouraging.

---

### The Niche Premium Principle

Mass-market platforms price low because they need volume. Caerus does not need volume — it needs a small number of the right customers.

A parent paying £50/month for Atom Learning to prepare their child for 11+ is paying because the stakes are high and the alternatives are worse. The same logic applies to CE Latin at 13+: the stakes are high (entrance to their chosen senior school), the available resources are thin, and parents who are already buying Galore Park books and paying for weekly tutors at £50–70/hour are not going to balk at £25–35 for a year's access to a well-built digital resource.

The risk is pricing too low. At £9.99/year, you look like a hobby project. At £24.99/year, you look like a cheap tool. At £34.99/year, you are in the range of "credible specialist product" — still much less than a single tutoring session, and parents will frame it that way.

---

### Recommended Pricing Structure

**Individual access (B2C — parents buying for their child):**

| Tier | Price | What it gets |
|---|---|---|
| Annual access | **£29.99/year** | Full access to all content, quizzes, grammar tools, AI tutor (Bob) |
| No monthly option | — | Monthly subscriptions invite cancellation after a term; annual is better for retention and cash flow |

Why £29.99 and not £34.99 or £24.99: It sits below the psychological £30 threshold while still signalling a proper product. It is exactly one tutoring session at the lower end of market rates. The framing writes itself: "Less than one tutoring session for a year's access."

Do not offer a free tier. A free tier creates a two-class product, requires you to decide what's gated, and undermines the premium positioning. If you want a trial, offer a 7-day free trial that requires payment details upfront — the Tassomai/Atom model. But given the current build stage, do not launch a trial mechanism on day one. Launch with paid access only and use Mumsnet/tutor word of mouth to drive it.

**School licence (B2B — Heads of Classics buying for their department):**

| Tier | Price | What it gets |
|---|---|---|
| Single school | **£200/year** | Unlimited pupil access for one school |
| Multi-school (3+) | **£150/year per school** | Volume discount for MATs or networks |

This is unchanged from the existing recommendation. The individual licence price (£29.99/year) is deliberately lower than school licences per-pupil at scale. A school with 15 CE Latin pupils paying £200/year is paying £13.33/pupil — significantly cheaper than individual access. This gives the school-pitch a compelling value case: "For less than the cost of one Galore Park book per pupil, every pupil gets a year's digital access."

**What about a free tier for Mowden?**
Your own school gets free access because they always have — via the Mowden site, which stays live. The Caerus site has no obligation to be free to anyone. If a Mowden parent finds the Caerus site and pays £29.99 for it, that is not a problem.

---

### One-Off vs Subscription vs Freemium: The Decision

**Recommendation: Annual subscription only, no freemium.**

**Why not one-off purchase:** CE Latin pupils are only in Year 8 for one year. A one-off purchase naturally works for a single-year product. However, subscription creates recurring revenue — if you sell 50 annual subscriptions this year, you have 50 renewal conversations next year. A one-off payment gives you nothing the following year.

**Why not monthly:** Monthly subscriptions in an academic context lead to parents buying for one term (September to Christmas) and cancelling. You want annual. The product aligns with the academic year naturally — sell in September, renewal in September.

**Why not freemium:** You do not have the content volume to make a freemium model work. Freemium only functions when the free tier is genuinely useful and the paid tier is clearly more useful. With a focused CE Latin product, the right move is "all the content, paid, reasonably priced." Freemium also creates support overhead (people arguing about what should be free) that you do not want.

**Exception:** A genuinely free "taster" on TES (a static resource, not access to the interactive site) is fine and serves as marketing. That is not a freemium tier — it is a sample.

---

### What Parents in the Independent Sector Are Already Paying

Mumsnet data and tutor rate discussions give us a reasonable picture:

- Private tutors: £35–75/hour; parents with CE pupils typically use tutors once or twice a week in Year 8, spending £1,500–3,000/year on tuition alone
- Galore Park revision guides: £12–18 each; most CE families buy 2–4 books per subject
- Atom Learning (11+ prep): £32–56/month annualised; many CE families use this at 11+ and are therefore accustomed to the spend
- Tassomai (for science in Year 8): £29.99/month
- Revision courses (short-stay, Easter holidays): £300–1,500 for residential CE courses

In this context, £29.99/year for a specialist CE Latin tool is not a difficult sell. It is a rounding error on what these families are already spending. The challenge is not price — it is awareness. Parents do not know the tool exists.

---

### Pricing Implementation Note

Do not implement a payment system on day one. The initial individual-access model can operate on a simple trust-and-email basis: parent emails caerusrevision@gmail.com, you send a payment link (Stripe, PayPal, or a simple checkout page), they pay, you send the URL. This is manual and does not scale, but it validates demand before you invest in building a proper checkout flow.

Once you have 10 paying individual users, build the checkout. Not before.
